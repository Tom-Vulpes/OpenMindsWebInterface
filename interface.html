Discussion questions

  1. Who is this person, really? What's their life like beyond this one card?
  2. What does this condition mean for them day to day?
  3. What is it about this environment that might cause difficulty?
  4. Is this actually a real challenge, or an assumption?
  5. What can't be changed about this situation? What can?

Remember

You cannot change the person. You cannot change the condition. But you can design the environment differently.

A theory about what is true — not an idea for a solution. "The environment is causing difficulty because..." not "We should build..."
Phase 3

Investigation

Before you build anything, test your hypothesis against reality. Is this actually true? Who experiences this? What has already been tried? Intellectual honesty happens here.

Your hypothesis will appear here once you've written it in Phase 2.

Is this actually true?

What evidence can you find that this challenge is real? What do people who live with it say?

Who experiences this?

How many people? In what contexts? Are there differences between groups?

What has already been tried?

What existing solutions, technologies, or approaches exist? What worked? What didn't, and why?

Does your hypothesis hold up?

After investigating, what's your verdict?

Why this phase matters

The most common failure in design is solving the wrong problem brilliantly. Investigation is what separates a real response from a clever gadget nobody needs.

Research strategies

  • Talk to people who are directly affected
  • Search for existing research or reports
  • Look for organisations working on this
  • Consider what stakeholders haven't been considered
  • Ask: who profits from this staying unsolved?

On AI use here

AI can help you find sources and explore existing knowledge — but you must evaluate what it returns. Don't accept AI summaries uncritically. Verify. Follow citations. Talk to actual people.

Phase 5

Reflection

The prototype is not the end point. What did you learn? What worked? What didn't? What would you do differently? Documenting the journey is itself a learning outcome.

What did you build?

Describe your prototype — not just what it does, but why you made the choices you did.

What worked?

What went better than expected? What did the technology enable that surprised you?

What didn't work?

Where did you get stuck? What did you try that failed? What would you do differently?

What did you learn about the challenge?

Not the technology — the societal challenge you were addressing. Did your understanding change?

What would you do next?

If you had another week, another month, or access to any resources — what's the next step?
